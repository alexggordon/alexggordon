## 2024 Plan

Plan is to do two styles of wine this year, rose and red. We plan to do individual fermentation of 

* Pinot Noir (PN) 
* Pinot Noir Rose (PNR)
* Barbera (B)
* Barbera Rose (BR)

These grapes were chosen because they were the highest quality grapes available to us. 

The yeasts we're using this year are:

* Rose: [Alchemy 1](https://www.lallemandwine.com/en/australia/products/wine-yeasts/anchor-alchemy-i)
* Barbera: [BM 4X4](https://scottlab.com/lalvin-bm-4x4-wine-yeast-bm4x4)
* Pinot Noir: RC212

We're mixing the yeast with Go-Ferm Sterol FLASH again, to allow speed up adding the yeasts. We'll also add Fermaid-K And Fermaid-O through the primary fermentation process. 

Be on the lookout for weird smells or colors. Wait for primary fermentation to pretty much stop before adding malolactic culture. Primary is done when specific gravity is less than 1.000.

Once primary fermentation has stopped, we'll press. The quality of the grapes this year will influence how long we keep the reds on the skins. We're thinking that if they're higher quality we'll keep them on the skins for longer. Once we've pressed, we'll do secondary femeration for PN and B. 

## 2024 Notes:

### Yeast

This was a concern and a problem with last years vintage, so I focused on it more this year.

The yeast selection this year doesn't exactly follow Scott Labs [recommendations](https://scottlabsltd.com/en-us/handbooks), however the recommendation came very highly from a winemaker at Musto. 

The BM 4X4 specifies:

> Sangiovese, Cabernet Sauvignon, Grenache, Zinfandel, Chardonnay, Italian reds

Barbera is an Italian Red, but it feels like an afterthought there. 

Additionally, BM 4X4 is of below average compatibility with MLF fermentation. 


Alchemy 1 specifies:

> Sauvignon Blanc, Chardonnay, Chenin Blanc, Riesling, Pinot Gris, Rhône whites, aromatic whites, cider

This also came at a strong recommendation to use with the Roses, which is why we chose it. However, if we make rose again I would like to try and use ICV SUNROSE.


### Débourbage

Depending on how the Roses end up, one thing we could try next time is débourbage, which would mean that on the day after pressing, before adding yeast, we would rack the Roses to a different tanks, with the goal of clearing the must of solid matters.

The solid matters can negatively affect taste. Some places, specifically in France, actually filter the must before adding yeast. If we're looking for a Provence style rose, one that drives for lighter color and a linear palette, we can evaluate doing this. 



### SO2 Addition timing

we added SO2 at the end of primary, because of an off smell.
